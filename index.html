Navigating Family Breakdown

In every Australian secondary classroom, 1 in 5 students is navigating family breakdown. This impacts academic performance through missed deadlines, poor concentration, and emotional withdrawal. During adolescence—a critical period of neuroplasticity—students face a 3-4 year gap between parental separation and divorce finalization, often coinciding with their secondary years.

Identifying Students in Need

Signs of distress often look similar to normal adolescent behavior. Key indicators include school refusal—different from truancy—which involves strong emotional distress about attending school.

Students may show internalizing behaviors (withdrawal, missed deadlines) or externalizing behaviors (defiance, aggression)—often reflecting the conflict they experience at home.

The challenges of living between two households can worsen these issues through logistical complexity.

The Developing Brain

During adolescence—critical period of neuroplasticity—the developing brain has heightened sensitivity to environmental stressors.

Research Finding: Higher parental negative emotion triggers increased activation in the adolescent amygdala (threat detection center), and constant emotional regulation exhausts regulatory resources needed for academic learning.

This neurobiological change means students often misinterpret cognitive deficits as lack of motivation, when they're actually experiencing physiological responses to trauma.

High-Risk Groups

Adolescents aged 15-17 report the highest distress levels (21%) during routine separations. Students with special educational needs, especially ASD and ADHD, face significantly compounded vulnerabilities.

For Students with ASD:

Predictable routines are disrupted, causing anxiety and sensory overload in dual-household environments.

Critical Barrier:

Communication breakdown creates systemic barriers to essential support services like IEPs and medication adjustments.

Why This Matters

Without intervention, these cognitive challenges can lead to academic failure, higher risk of early school leaving, and long-term educational disadvantage.

The Educator's Role: Your understanding of these neurobiological realities transforms how you interpret behavior—seeing signs of trauma rather than disengagement, and providing the right support at the right time.

Every intervention matters—your classroom can be the safe space that makes the difference.

Furthermore, the temporal trends of separation are particularly relevant for secondary educators.

While the median duration from marriage to divorce in Australia is 13.2 years, the median duration to final separation is shorter, at approximately 9 years.

This indicates a gap of 3–4 years between the breakdown of the relationship and the legal finalisation of divorce.

This period also aligns with a time of higher neurobiological vulnerability.

Health professionals also report more concerns about the wellbeing of 15–17-year-olds than other children.

Because of this, secondary schools become an important place where the complex impacts of family breakdown appear.

Impact on Development, Learning, and Neurobiology

How Family Breakdown Affects Adolescent Development

The impact of family breakdown on adolescents goes beyond immediate behaviours. It can fundamentally alter neurobiological development and cognitive functioning.

Adolescence is a critical period of neuroplasticity where the brain is highly sensitive to environmental influences such as parenting.

However, this period is also characterised by significant structural remodeling, specifically an imbalance between the developing limbic system and the still-maturing prefrontal cortex.

The Neurological Impact of Stress

This neurological state makes students acutely sensitive to environmental stressors.

Evidence suggests that the adolescent stress-response involves an increased secretion of cortisol, and prolonged exposure to family conflict can lead to sustained cortisol secretion, which is a neurobiological marker for depression and other psychological dysfunctions.

Consequences for Executive Function

Consequently, chronic adversity can disrupt the prefrontal cortex—the area controlling executive functions such as impulse control, planning, and emotional regulation which reduces a student's capacity to engage academically.

Source: Podwinski & Laletas, 2025

Parent-Child Brain Connection

There is a clear link between parents' emotional states and adolescents' brain responses.

Turpyn et al. (2017) found that higher levels of parental negative emotion are linked to increased activation of the adolescent amygdala, the brain's threat detection centre, when adolescents are exposed to negative stimuli.

Amygdala Activation

Higher levels of parental negative emotion are linked to increased activation of the adolescent amygdala, the brain's threat detection centre, when adolescents are exposed to negative stimuli.

Source: Turpyn et al., 2017

The Role of Parental Emotion Regulation

Importantly, this effect depends on how well parents regulate their own emotions.

When parents struggle with emotion regulation, adolescents show increased activity not only in the amygdala but also in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC).

Not Unregulated, Over-Engaged

This higher vmPFC activity does not mean adolescents are unregulated; instead, it reflects over-engagement of regulatory resources as they try to manage intense emotional arousal.

Over time, this constant neural effort can exhaust their regulatory capacity, leaving adolescents biologically "primed" to detect threats and reducing the cognitive resources available for learning.

Source: Turpyn et al., 2017

Educational Consequences

This neurobiological change affects educational outcomes.

The mental effort needed to manage this heightened arousal uses resources required for complex academic tasks.

A Critical Misunderstanding

Therefore, deficits in executive function caused by family stress can be misunderstood by educators as lack of motivation or ability, instead of a physiological response to trauma.

Without proper intervention, these cognitive challenges can lead to academic failure, higher risk of early school leaving, and long-term educational disadvantage.

Source: Clark, 2023

Impact on Students with Special Needs

ASD/ADHD

The negative effects of family breakdown are more significant for students with special educational needs, especially those with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) and Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD).

Students with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)

For students with ASD, having predictable routines is important for emotional regulation, but parental separation often brings changes in households and unpredictable schedules, disrupting these routines.

As a result, students may show more self-stimulatory behaviours, sensory seeking, or withdrawal.

Important Understanding

Importantly, these behaviours are not just disruptive. They often help students cope with the disruption and should not be discouraged without first providing alternative ways to regulate emotions.

Source: Hetherington, 2017

Challenges of Living Between Two Households

The challenges of living between two households create particular risks for neurodiverse students, as inconsistencies in routines and sensory environments can trigger anxiety and overload.

Schools try to address this through "reasonable adjustments," but recent research shows that these adjustments only work well when there is strong collaboration in the "Communication between Parent-Teacher-Child" domain.

Source: Page et al., 2024

Communication Breakdown

In high-conflict divorces, this communication often breaks down, leading to what teachers call "communication uncertainty".

Teachers frequently face unclear situations about which parents can make key decisions, such as regarding medication or Individualised Education Plans (IEPs), causing delays or loss of essential support.

From Private Issue to System Barrier

As a result, failures in parental communication move beyond a private family issue and become a systemic barrier that hinders the student's access to fair education and academic success.

Sources: Page et al., 2024; Podwinski & Laletas, 2025

Whole-School and Pedagogical Approaches

Trauma-Informed Teaching

The Berry Street Education Model (BSEM)

To reduce the negative effects of family breakdown, secondary schools need to go beyond reactive discipline and adopt proactive, trauma-informed teaching approaches.

The Berry Street Education Model (BSEM) provides a framework for students who may be disengaged or disruptive due to trauma, supporting them to "heal first, then thrive".

Foundation Domains

  • • Body: Helps students build physical regulation
  • • Relationship: Establishes relational safety

Source: Bailey & Brunzell, 2019

Growth Domains

  • • Stamina: Focuses on resilience and emotional intelligence to support academic persistence
  • • Engagement: Provides strategies for students who have withdrawn psychologically, helping them stay motivated despite challenges at home

Source: Bailey & Brunzell, 2019

Classroom Practice and Reasonable Adjustments

For students with special needs experiencing family separation, teaching strategies must be translated into practical "reasonable adjustments" to ensure fair access to education.

Proactive vs Reactive Approaches

Research shows that schools often apply these adjustments reactively, but supporting students effectively requires proactive measures to reduce logistical challenges across households.

Source: Page et al., 2024

Examples of Proactive Adjustments

  • • Providing duplicate textbooks or digital licenses to prevent anxiety when materials are forgotten during custody transitions
  • • Enhanced visual schedules, such as interactive magnetic routine charts, to map custody arrangements and reduce uncertainty for students with ASD
  • • Teacher aides to help students remain included and maintain continuity in the classroom

Key Considerations

  • • Students with ASD find dual-household living unpredictable
  • • Visual schedules help reduce uncertainty
  • • Consistency between households is ideal but often challenging

Sources: Hetherington, 2017; Page et al., 2024

Parent Partnerships and Legal Obligations

Supporting students affected by family breakdown requires managing complex and sometimes adversarial parental relationships while strictly following legal requirements.

The School as a Safe Space

Educators must remain neutral, keeping the school a "demilitarized" safe space rather than a place to resolve family conflicts.

Legal Requirements

Legally, unless a court order says otherwise, both parents usually share parental responsibility.

This means non-residential parents generally have the right to attend school events and receive documents like school reports, a detail often misunderstood in high-conflict situations.

Source: NSW Government, 2021

Complex Situations

We also face "grey areas" when third parties are involved.

For example, step-parents collecting a child from school need explicit consent from the biological parent, adding complexity to communication.

Finally, while schools do not enforce Family Court orders, we must check the terms of any Apprehended Violence Orders (AVOs) to protect student safety. At the same time, we must balance confidentiality with the duty to support distressed students.

Sources: NSW Government, 2021; Podwinski & Laletas, 2025

Connecting to Community Support

Understanding Professional Boundaries

I understand my limits as an educator.

I do not have the ability to fix a student's home life or act as a therapist.

My role is to act as a bridge. When a student's needs go beyond what I can manage, such as severe anxiety or safety concerns. I will report the case to the school student wellbeing team, the Principal etc.

Specialized Adjustments for ASD Students

For students with ASD, who find dual-household living unpredictable, standard supports must be adapted:

Visual Custody Mapping

Interactive magnetic routine charts to map custody arrangements and reduce uncertainty (Hetherington, 2017).

Functional Behavior Response

Understand that stimming behaviors serve functional purposes—attempts to regulate anxiety (Hetherington, 2017).

Retreat Space

Provide designated quiet places for managing overwhelming emotions (Hetherington, 2017).

The Role of Teacher Aides

The success of these adjustments often depends on teacher aides, whose support helps students remain included and maintain learning continuity in mainstream classrooms (Page et al., 2024).
